The Paradigm Shift

The "Time Compaction" Hypothesis

Survival in nature requires real-time decision-making in highly dynamic, time-constrained situations. How does the brain process this overwhelming amount of spatiotemporal information?

The CIR (Compact Internal Representation) Project proposes a paradigm shift: the brain does not encode time explicitly. Instead, it transforms time into space by predicting future interactions and mapping them as static obstacles or targets.

By reducing complex dynamic scenarios into static spatial maps, we explain how living beings achieve fast, reliable, and optimal decision-making.

Research Areas

The Three Pillars

Our interdisciplinary research combines neuroscience, mathematics, physics, and computational modeling to understand how the brain represents dynamic environments, predicts future interactions, and supports adaptive behavior.

Cognitive Neuroscience

Biological mechanisms of spatiotemporal cognition

Understanding how biological systems represent and predict dynamic environments.

We investigate Compact Internal Representations through behavioral experiments and neurophysiological recordings. Our research spans human cognition and animal electrophysiology, including hippocampal dynamics and place cell activity, to uncover how predictive information is encoded in neural populations.

Mathematical Modeling

Computational principles of cognition

Developing mathematical frameworks to describe the dynamics underlying cognition.

We build models that connect biological mechanisms with cognitive functions. Using dynamical systems, neural models, and complex network approaches, we study how neural circuits generate predictions and organize information into cognitive maps.

Cognitive Robotics

From biological principles to artificial systems

Exploring how principles of biological cognition can inspire artificial systems.

We implement theoretical models in autonomous systems to investigate navigation, interaction, and decision-making in complex environments, bridging neuroscience and computational approaches.
